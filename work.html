On-Screen Legibility

You're doing work for me. Right now. You've been randomly presented with one of three different stylesheets. The only variance between these style sheets are the background & text colours.

Why, you ask? Eye-strain. Aren't you tired of reading black text on a bright white lightbulb? Have you purchased "computer glasses" or blue-blocker already? Are your eyeballs drying-out, shriviling to one tenth of their natural size, from your unblinking dedication to reading on a computer screen?

I am. I have. Mine Do. I want a better reading & legibility experience on a computer screen. I think there is a better option to create that perceived contrast, I think there is an option c. Help me find it.

Markov Chain Website

During the 6th week of the General Assembly UXDI Immersive Program, we were discussing visual design in user experience, & thus having been bequeathed an ultimatum,

“The purpose of visual design is to create a positive user experience through a usable and pleasurable user interface,”

I decided that needed to be challenged.

What about the opposite? What about a negative user experience (through an unusable & disagreeable interface) & what would it’s purpose be?

The discussion resulted in no examples & the blasé conclusion that as long as the experience/interface is being design to complete a goal, the design has accomplished its purpose. Even if the design is difficult to use, & creates a poor user experience, by accomplishing the goal, it becomes an example of ‘good’ user experience design.

The theme of the discussion was benevolent v. nefarious design, but it got the old gears churning. How would one create a user experience that is has a maleficent intent, for a negative purpose?

A Markov Chain Website.

If you’re not familiar with Markov chains, Wikipedia Markov chains. If that didn’t make any sense, the basic idea is that a Markov chain models the progression through states & describes the probability of state change in a system wherein any state can be reached from any other state, within a set of conditions.

Looking to learn more?

Markov chains mathmatically describe the probability of a series of events, each with a condition.Wikipedia has a wealth of Examples of Markov Chains.

The Site.

One page, 3 buttons: ‘A’ ‘B’ ‘C.’ Clicking ‘A’ presents the same set of buttons, but in a well design user experience. Clicking ‘B’ presents them, but manipulates the user into selecting a certain option. Selecting ‘C’ presents the options, but randomizes to which state each directs the user.

Important to the idea of progress is some user interaction. Perhaps a 99¢ payment; maybe just a captcha form: some action that is intrinsically or at least practically, irreversible.

What’s the point?

The point is that I (the creator of the site) am not defining the goal, & therefore, there can be an intrinsically bad user-experience, not just a crappy design that doesn’t accomplish a goal. It removes morality from the user experience design, again, allowing for a truly negative user experience. It’s also not a loop, so the user is, in fact, progressing, just without an end goal.

The result.

Perhaps all this will accomplish is an artistic statement on life, or a magnificent thought experiment. I am hoping it will provide a place to start exploring definitive ‘bad’ user experience design.
